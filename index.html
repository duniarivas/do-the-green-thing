Manly is the New Green

We want to put the spotlight on the most significant gender difference between consumption habits in an environmental context: men’s higher spending on petrol and diesel for their cars/motorbikes.

When highlighting that, we want to link it to the concept of toxic masculinity and make it as a clear example of the research that shows that “men may shun eco-friendly behavior because of what it conveys about their masculinity”.
We want to challenge men’s ideas about masculinity and make them start asking themselves questions about it.

This is Ivan.

Ivan loves riding his motorbike, but recent headlines about weather disasters has given him a sense of unease he just can’t ignore. Now, he wants to do the right thing and is thrilled to start his new eco-friendly lifestyle with the bicycle he just bought.

One thought does bother him though... will his biker friends call him a p*ssy? Will a bicycle kill that badass manly vibe that the motorbike gives him? Does he dare to step out of the macho zone?

  • Down Arrow (light)

Unfortunately, Ivan is not the only one struggling with these questions. Research has shown that men may put their eco-friendly ambitions aside due to the fear of being seen as less masculine and more ”girly”. At the same time, it’s stated that men’s lifestyles are leaving a larger carbon footprint than women’s. In line with Ivan’s love for motorbikes, the biggest difference between men and women’s spending habits is men’s higher consumption of gas and diesel. Together with the ideal of being macho, this creates a toxic cycle that has serious negative impact on the environment.

So, how to break this cycle? We believe that putting the spotlight on this topic and encouraging men to question their habits is a great way to start. What will happen if Ivan follows his heart and decides to go with the bicycle? Let’s find out below!
